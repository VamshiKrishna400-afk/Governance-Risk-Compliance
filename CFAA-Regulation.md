# Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

# What is the CFAA?

The **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)** is a U.S. federal law that prohibits unauthorized access to computers and computer systems. It is one of the primary laws used to prosecute cybercrimes such as hacking, malware distribution, password theft, and computer fraud.

- **Enacted:** 1986
- **Country:** United States
- **Primary Purpose:** Prevent unauthorized access to computers and protect digital information and computer systems.

---

# Who Does the CFAA Apply To?

The CFAA applies to:

- Individuals
- Employees
- Hackers
- Contractors
- Businesses
- Government agencies

It protects:

- Government computers
- Financial institution systems
- Healthcare systems
- Educational institutions
- Cloud servers
- Personal computers connected to the Internet
- Business networks

### Example

> A hacker gains unauthorized access to a company's internal server. This is a violation of the CFAA.

---

# What is a Protected Computer?

A **protected computer** is any computer covered by the CFAA.

Examples include:

- Government computers
- Bank servers
- Hospital systems
- Business servers
- Cloud platforms
- Internet-connected computers
- Mobile devices connected to the Internet

### Example

> A cloud-hosted company database accessible over the Internet is considered a protected computer.

---

# Unauthorized Access

The CFAA makes it illegal to:

- Access a computer without permission
- Bypass authentication controls
- Use stolen passwords
- Access restricted systems
- Continue accessing systems after authorization has been revoked

### Example

> An employee whose account has been disabled logs in using a coworker's password. This is unauthorized access.

---

# Exceeding Authorized Access

A person may violate the CFAA if they access information they are **not permitted to access**, even if they have legitimate access to the system.

Examples:

- Viewing confidential employee records
- Accessing payroll information without authorization
- Opening customer databases outside job responsibilities

### Example

> A customer support employee accesses executive salary records without permission.

---

# Computer Fraud

The CFAA prohibits using computers to commit fraud, including:

- Identity theft
- Financial fraud
- Credit card fraud
- Online scams
- Data manipulation for personal gain

### Example

> A criminal uses stolen banking credentials to transfer money from a victim's account.

---

# Theft of Information

It is illegal to steal confidential information from computer systems, including:

- Trade secrets
- Customer databases
- Financial records
- Medical information
- Government information
- Intellectual property

### Example

> An employee downloads a company's confidential customer database before joining a competitor.

---

# Malware and Harmful Programs

The CFAA prohibits creating or distributing software intended to damage computers, including:

- Viruses
- Worms
- Ransomware
- Trojans
- Spyware

### Example

> A cybercriminal sends ransomware that encrypts a hospital's patient records and demands payment.

---

# Damage to Computer Systems

It is illegal to intentionally damage computer systems by:

- Deleting files
- Destroying databases
- Disrupting services
- Corrupting software
- Launching destructive attacks

### Example

> A former employee deletes company databases before leaving the organization.

---

# Denial-of-Service (DoS/DDoS) Attacks

The CFAA prohibits attacks that intentionally make computer systems unavailable.

Examples:

- Denial-of-Service (DoS)
- Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS)

### Example

> Attackers flood an online shopping website with millions of requests, making it unavailable to customers.

---

# Password Trafficking

It is illegal to:

- Sell stolen passwords
- Buy stolen credentials
- Share passwords for unauthorized access

### Example

> A hacker sells administrator passwords on an underground marketplace.

---

# Extortion Using Computers

The CFAA prohibits using computers to extort money or other benefits by threatening to:

- Damage systems
- Delete data
- Leak confidential information
- Launch cyberattacks

### Example

> An attacker demands cryptocurrency in exchange for not publishing stolen customer records.

---

# Common CFAA Violations

Examples include:

- Hacking into company systems
- Guessing or stealing passwords
- Installing malware
- Stealing confidential data
- Launching ransomware attacks
- Conducting DDoS attacks
- Accessing systems after employment ends
- Modifying or deleting company data without authorization

### Example

> A former contractor uses old credentials to access confidential company files after their contract has ended.

---

# Penalties for CFAA Violations

Depending on the offense, penalties may include:

- Criminal fines
- Civil lawsuits
- Restitution to victims
- Probation
- Imprisonment
- Seizure of equipment used in the crime

The severity of the penalty depends on:

- Intent
- Financial damage caused
- Number of victims
- Whether it is a repeat offense
- Impact on critical infrastructure

### Example

> A hacker who compromises a bank's systems and steals customer information may face substantial fines and imprisonment.

---

# Best Practices to Avoid CFAA Violations

Organizations and individuals should:

- Access only systems they are authorized to use
- Follow company security policies
- Use strong authentication
- Never share passwords
- Keep software updated
- Report security incidents promptly
- Obtain written permission before performing security testing

### Example

> A penetration tester obtains written authorization before testing a company's network for vulnerabilities.
